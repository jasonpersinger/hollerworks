# HOLLER.WORKS Brand Guide

This guide is meant to keep HOLLER.WORKS consistent as the site, social presence, and launch materials grow.

The goal is not to make the brand slick in a generic startup way. The goal is to make it feel distinct, trustworthy, and marketable while preserving the things that already make it memorable.

## Brand Core

### Brand Name

- Display / wordmark: `HOLLER.WORKS`
- Editorial / body copy / link-like usage: `holler.works`
- Avoid using `Holler.Works` as the default form

### Descriptor

Use a plain descriptor when needed:

`a moderated job board for tech and tech-adjacent work in Appalachia`

### Positioning

HOLLER.WORKS is a focused, human-moderated job board for tech and tech-adjacent work connected to Appalachia.

It should feel:

- practical
- regional without being provincial
- internet-native without being trend-chasing
- human and trustworthy
- a little strange in a good way

### Tone Summary

The brand should feel `utilitarian but stylish`, with a healthy appreciation for the old web.

That means:

- direct over polished
- memorable over generic
- warm over corporate
- structured over flashy
- regional character over interchangeable “tech platform” language

## Brand Pillars

### 1. Human Moderation

This is not an anonymous dump of scraped jobs. The brand should consistently signal that a real person is paying attention.

### 2. Appalachian Specificity

Appalachia is not backdrop copy. It is part of the reason the product exists.

### 3. Useful Over Performative

Everything should feel like it was built to be used, not just admired.

### 4. Old-Web Literacy

The brand can reference the old web through typography, layout logic, pixel treatment, and blunt interface language. It should not become retro cosplay.

## Personality

### What We Are

- sharp
- grounded
- internet-literate
- regional
- independent
- low-ego
- curated

### What We Are Not

- sterile
- venture-slick
- faux-folksy
- ironic for its own sake
- overloaded with nostalgia gimmicks
- “future of work” jargon-heavy

## Voice and Copy

### Voice Rules

- Write plainly.
- Keep sentences tight.
- Prefer clarity over hype.
- Sound like a real person, not a campaign.
- Use regional context carefully and confidently, not as costume.
- Let the product’s usefulness do the selling.

### Lowercase Usage

Lowercase is an approved part of the HOLLER.WORKS voice, but it should be used intentionally.

Use more lowercase in:

- branding copy
- launch copy
- social posts
- editorial/about-style writing
- campaign headers and supporting lines

Keep standard sentence case in:

- core product UI
- forms
- system messages
- instructions
- trust and policy language
- anything legal, transactional, or operationally sensitive

Reason:

Lowercase gives the brand a grounded, internet-native, anti-corporate feel. Used too broadly, it can make a jobs product feel less credible than it needs to be. The right split is:

- `marketing/editorial voice` can lean lowercase
- `product/system voice` should stay crisp and conventional

### Good Copy Traits

- concrete
- restrained
- slightly gritty
- calm
- credible

### Avoid

- “revolutionizing hiring”
- “connecting talent ecosystems”
- “empowering the future of work”
- empty startup optimism
- overexplaining the joke or aesthetic

### Example Voice

Good:

`At holler.works, the focus is tech and tech-adjacent work connected to Appalachia.`

Good:

`Every listing is reviewed by a human before it goes live.`

Bad:

`We are building a next-generation talent platform for the Appalachian innovation economy.`

## Taglines and Phrases

### Core Supporting Language

- `// appalachia`
- `// LAUNCHING HOLLER.WORKS`
- `STAY.`
- `BUILD.`
- `moderated tech jobs for appalachia`

### Usage Guidance

- `// appalachia` works best as a visual or supporting line, not a headline substitute everywhere.
- The `//` construction can also be used as a branded header treatment for launches, announcements, and social posts.
- `STAY. BUILD.` should be used sparingly and with confidence.
- Do not stack too many slogans in one surface.

### `//` Header Device

The `//` prefix is part of the HOLLER.WORKS visual language.

It should signal:

- a clear announcement
- a system message
- a launch or milestone
- a blunt, internet-native headline

Good examples:

- `// appalachia`
- `// launching holler.works`
- `// new jobs this week`
- `// now posting`

Usage rules:

- keep it short
- keep it uppercase or near-uppercase for display use
- use it as a header, kicker, or opening line
- pair it well with monospace or terminal-like typography
- do not use it on every line of copy

### Social Header Formula

A strong HOLLER.WORKS social structure is:

1. `//` header
2. short supporting line
3. plainspoken body copy

Example:

`// LAUNCHING HOLLER.WORKS`

`>> stay. build. connected.`

`appalachia’s dedicated tech job board is now live. we're connecting local builders with regional companies. no fluff. no hustle. just the holler.`

This is also a good place to lean into lowercase body copy, because the goal is voice and atmosphere rather than operational instruction.

## Visual Direction

### Design Principle

The visual system should feel like a useful tool that happens to have taste.

The right balance is:

- black terminal energy
- rust-orange urgency
- green Appalachian grounding
- pixel-era references used as texture, not as clutter

### Overall Look

- dark background
- high contrast text
- monospace foundation
- simple geometry
- visible borders and rails
- sparse, deliberate accent color

## Color Palette

Retain the existing palette.

### Primary Colors

- `Black`: `#0E0E0E`
- `Rust`: `#F28927`
- `Appalachia Green`: `#39803E`
- `Gray`: `#AAAAAA`
- `Light Gray`: `#CCCCCC`

### Functional Roles

- `Black` is the canvas.
- `Rust` is the primary action/accent color.
- `Green` is for regional identity, labels, and secondary emphasis.
- `Light Gray` is for key text and wordmark emphasis.
- `Gray` is for body copy and interface texture.

### Color Guidance

- Keep rust dominant over green.
- Use green as grounding, not decoration overload.
- Do not introduce extra bright accent colors unless there is a functional reason.
- Avoid soft pastel versions of the palette.
- Avoid gradients that dilute the brand’s hard-edged feel.

## Logo System

### Primary Mark

The preferred primary mark is the full lockup:

- pixel mascot above or beside the wordmark
- `HOLLER.WORKS` in rust
- supporting `// appalachia` in green

Standard files:

- `assets/brand/logo/hollerworks-lockup-primary.png` for the default on-black branded treatment
- `assets/brand/logo/hollerworks-lockup-master.png` for the transparent master export

Use the primary lockup when the brand needs to identify itself clearly on its own.

### Wordmark

The wordmark should remain strong, condensed, and high-contrast.

It works because it feels:

- bold
- legible
- slightly industrial
- easy to recognize at a glance

Standard files:

- `assets/brand/logo/hollerworks-wordmark-primary.png` for the default on-black branded treatment
- `assets/brand/logo/hollerworks-wordmark-master.png` for the transparent master export

Use the wordmark-only mark when the full lockup is too tall or visually heavy, but the brand name still needs to read clearly.

### Pixel Mascot

The pixel mascot is a real asset, not a throwaway flourish.

It adds:

- memorability
- internet-native character
- old-web / game-era sensibility
- friendliness without softness

Standard files:

- `assets/brand/logo/hollerworks-mascot-master.png` for the transparent master export
- `assets/brand/logo/hollerworks-mascot-on-black.png` for the default on-black display version

### Mascot Guidance

- Keep it crisp and pixelated.
- Do not anti-alias or blur it.
- Do not overanimate it.
- Do not turn it into a meme on every surface.
- Use it as a signature asset, especially in social, hero, and launch materials.
- The mascot is an approved secondary mark and can stand alone in UI, favicon, avatar, and decorative placements.

### Approved Variants

- `Primary lockup`: full logo with mascot, `HOLLER.WORKS`, and `// appalachia`
- `Wordmark-only`: `HOLLER.WORKS` in the approved rust treatment
- `Mascot-only`: the pixel mascot on black
- `Social banner`: wide campaign/banner asset for previews and headers
- `Mountain strip`: supporting texture, not a logo

Do not create alternate logo versions casually. If a new variant is needed, add it to the asset package deliberately.

## Logo Usage Rules

### Preferred Background

- use on black or near-black backgrounds

At the moment, black or near-black is the only approved default background treatment. The transparent masters exist for flexibility and production use, but they should still usually be placed on dark backgrounds unless a deliberate alternative treatment is designed.

### Preferred Treatments

- rust wordmark
- green supporting line
- original mascot colors

### Clear Space

- `Primary lockup`: keep at least 10% of the logo width clear on all sides
- `Mascot-only`: keep at least one eye-block of clear space around the sprite

If there is any doubt, give the mark more room. The brand looks better slightly undersold than crowded.

### Minimum Size

- `Primary lockup`: do not use below `220px` wide in digital layouts
- `Mascot-only`: `32px` is the practical minimum in product UI, `64px+` is preferred in marketing use
- `Favicon / app icons`: use the standardized icon exports rather than scaling ad hoc

### Mascot-Only Usage

Mascot-only is appropriate for:

- favicons and app icons
- avatars
- small UI placements
- decorative brand moments
- teasers where the full wordmark appears nearby

Use the full lockup when the logo must introduce or identify the brand without other context.

### Wordmark-Only Usage

Wordmark-only is appropriate for:

- narrow headers and footers
- sponsorship placements
- email and document headers
- merch or layouts where the mascot would feel too busy
- contexts where the brand name needs to stay the focus

Do not restyle, retype, or rebuild the wordmark manually. Use the standardized export.

### Avoid

- placing the logo on busy photo backgrounds
- recoloring the mascot arbitrarily
- stretching or smoothing the pixel art
- adding drop shadows or glow effects
- placing too many decorative elements around the mark
- rebuilding the logo from scratch in another font
- using the mountain strip as a substitute for the logo

## Typography

### Brand Typography Direction

The current monospace system is part of the identity and should remain central.

Current foundation:

- `'Courier New', Courier, monospace`

### Typographic Personality

- terminal-like
- workmanlike
- readable
- slightly nostalgic

### Usage Guidance

- Keep monospace as the dominant brand/system voice.
- Bold, condensed uppercase works well for major display treatments.
- Use size, spacing, and case for emphasis rather than decorative fonts.

### Avoid

- trendy SaaS sans-serif replacements as the main brand voice
- whimsical script or “craft” fonts
- overly polished editorial serif systems that fight the product

## UI Style Rules

### Interface Character

The UI should feel like:

- a useful board
- a small system with taste
- a place that respects the user’s time

### Keep

- rails
- borders
- dense but readable layouts
- inline system language
- obvious states
- tactile buttons
- straightforward labels

### Avoid

- glossy cards everywhere
- oversized whitespace that kills the old-web utility feel
- rounded, bubbly components
- generic startup illustration systems
- too much motion

## Imagery

### Good Imagery

- pixel art
- dark banners
- ridge / mountain silhouettes
- simple iconography
- crisp exports with strong contrast

### Avoid

- stock-office photography
- generic remote-work laptop imagery
- blue-gradient corporate visuals
- excessive mockup polish that clashes with the site

## Social and Marketing Use

### Social Presence

Social graphics should keep the brand recognizable immediately:

- black field
- rust wordmark
- green regional line
- mascot or ridge texture when useful

### Social Copy Tone

- concise
- specific
- human
- lightly rough-edged

The `//` header device should be used often enough to feel recognizable, but not so often that every post becomes the same shape.

### Good Launch Framing

- moderated
- tech-focused
- Appalachia-specific
- compensation required
- free to post during rollout

## Practical Do / Don't

### Do

- keep the palette intact
- keep the monospace foundation
- preserve the pixel mascot
- write like a person
- keep the brand a little rough around the edges
- favor usefulness over polish theater

### Don't

- sand the brand down until it looks like every other tech jobs product
- overplay “Appalachia” in a performative way
- replace the old-web references with generic modern UI language
- confuse weirdness with identity
- introduce random new colors or fonts casually

## Brand Standard for New Work

When evaluating a new page, social post, or asset, ask:

1. Does this still look like HOLLER.WORKS at a glance?
2. Does it preserve the black/rust/green system?
3. Does it feel useful first and stylish second?
4. Does it sound human?
5. Does the old-web influence feel intentional rather than gimmicky?
6. Would this still feel credible to an employer or job seeker who has never heard of the site?

If the answer to most of those is yes, it is probably on-brand.
